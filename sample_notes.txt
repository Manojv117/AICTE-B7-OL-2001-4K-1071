BIOLOGY STUDY NOTES — Chapter 5: Cell Biology and Genetics

=======================================================
PART 1: CELL STRUCTURE
=======================================================

The cell is the basic structural and functional unit of all living organisms.
There are two fundamental types of cells: prokaryotic and eukaryotic.

Prokaryotic Cells:
- Found in bacteria and archaea
- No membrane-bound nucleus; DNA is located in the nucleoid region
- Smaller in size (1–10 micrometers)
- No membrane-bound organelles
- Reproduce by binary fission
- Examples: E. coli, Streptococcus

Eukaryotic Cells:
- Found in plants, animals, fungi, and protists
- Contain a membrane-bound nucleus housing the DNA
- Larger in size (10–100 micrometers)
- Contain membrane-bound organelles (mitochondria, ER, Golgi, etc.)
- Reproduce by mitosis (for growth) or meiosis (for reproduction)

Key Organelles and Their Functions:
1. Nucleus: Control center of the cell; contains DNA and directs protein synthesis
2. Mitochondria: Powerhouse of the cell; produces ATP via cellular respiration
3. Ribosomes: Site of protein synthesis; found free in cytoplasm or on rough ER
4. Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER):
   - Rough ER: has ribosomes; involved in protein processing
   - Smooth ER: no ribosomes; involved in lipid synthesis and detoxification
5. Golgi Apparatus: Packages and ships proteins to their destinations
6. Lysosomes: Contain digestive enzymes; break down waste materials
7. Chloroplasts (plant cells only): Site of photosynthesis; contain chlorophyll
8. Cell Membrane: Phospholipid bilayer controlling what enters and exits the cell
9. Cell Wall (plant cells): Rigid outer layer made of cellulose; provides support
10. Vacuoles: Storage organelles; large central vacuole in plant cells maintains turgor pressure

=======================================================
PART 2: CELL DIVISION
=======================================================

MITOSIS:
Mitosis is the process of somatic (body) cell division producing two genetically
identical daughter cells. It is essential for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.

Phases of Mitosis:
1. Prophase — Chromosomes condense; spindle fibers form; nuclear envelope breaks down
2. Metaphase — Chromosomes align at the cell's equator (metaphase plate)
3. Anaphase — Sister chromatids separate; pulled to opposite poles by spindle fibers
4. Telophase — Nuclear envelopes reform; chromosomes de-condense
5. Cytokinesis — Cytoplasm divides; two daughter cells form

Before mitosis, the cell undergoes INTERPHASE (G1 → S phase → G2):
- G1: Cell grows; proteins synthesized
- S Phase: DNA replication occurs (chromosome number doubles)
- G2: Cell prepares for division; final checks

MEIOSIS:
Meiosis produces four genetically unique haploid cells (gametes).
It involves two rounds of division: Meiosis I and Meiosis II.
Key difference from mitosis: homologous chromosomes separate in Meiosis I,
and crossing-over during prophase I generates genetic variation.

=======================================================
PART 3: GENETICS
=======================================================

DNA Structure:
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid) is a double helix made of nucleotides.
Each nucleotide contains:
- A deoxyribose sugar
- A phosphate group
- A nitrogenous base: Adenine (A), Thymine (T), Guanine (G), or Cytosine (C)

Base pairing rules: A pairs with T (2 hydrogen bonds); G pairs with C (3 hydrogen bonds)

DNA Replication (occurs in S phase):
1. Helicase unwinds the double helix at the origin of replication
2. Primase adds RNA primers
3. DNA Polymerase III adds complementary nucleotides (5' to 3' direction)
4. DNA Polymerase I removes RNA primers; fills gaps
5. Ligase seals the sugar-phosphate backbone

Protein Synthesis:
The Central Dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

Transcription (DNA → mRNA):
- Occurs in the nucleus
- RNA Polymerase reads the template strand (3' to 5')
- mRNA is synthesized (5' to 3')
- mRNA is processed: 5' cap added, poly-A tail added, introns spliced out
- mRNA leaves nucleus through nuclear pores

Translation (mRNA → Protein):
- Occurs at ribosomes
- tRNA carries amino acids; anticodon base-pairs with mRNA codon
- Start codon: AUG (methionine)
- Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA
- Peptide bonds form between amino acids
- Polypeptide chain folds into a 3D protein structure

Mendelian Genetics:
Gregor Mendel's laws:
1. Law of Segregation: Alleles separate during gamete formation
2. Law of Independent Assortment: Genes on different chromosomes assort independently

Key Terms:
- Genotype: Genetic makeup (e.g., Aa, BB, rr)
- Phenotype: Observable characteristic
- Dominant allele: Expressed when one or two copies present
- Recessive allele: Only expressed when two copies present (homozygous recessive)
- Heterozygous: Two different alleles (Aa)
- Homozygous: Two identical alleles (AA or aa)
- Punnett Square: Tool to predict offspring ratios

Example: Tall (T) is dominant over short (t)
Cross: Tt × Tt → TT : Tt : tt = 1 : 2 : 1
Phenotype ratio: 3 tall : 1 short

Mutations:
A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence.
Types:
- Point mutation: Single nucleotide change (substitution, insertion, deletion)
- Frameshift mutation: Insertion or deletion causing a reading frame shift
- Chromosomal mutation: Large-scale change (deletion, duplication, inversion, translocation)

Mutations can be silent (no effect), missense (different amino acid), or nonsense (premature stop codon).

PART 4: PHOTOSYNTHESIS
=======================================================

Photosynthesis: 6CO2 + 6H2O + light energy → C6H12O6 + 6O2

Occurs in chloroplasts in two stages:

Light-Dependent Reactions (Thylakoid membrane):
- Light energy absorbed by chlorophyll
- Water is split (photolysis): 2H2O → 4H+ + 4e- + O2
- ATP synthesized via chemiosmosis (ATP synthase)
- NADPH formed from NADP+ + H+
- Products: ATP, NADPH, O2

Calvin Cycle / Light-Independent Reactions (Stroma):
- CO2 fixed by RuBisCO onto RuBP (5-carbon molecule)
- Uses ATP and NADPH from light reactions
- Produces G3P (glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate), which builds glucose
- RuBP is regenerated (3 turns needed to make 1 G3P net)

=======================================================
PART 5: CELLULAR RESPIRATION
=======================================================

Cellular Respiration: C6H12O6 + 6O2 → 6CO2 + 6H2O + ~38 ATP

Stage 1 — Glycolysis (cytoplasm):
- Glucose (6C) split into 2 pyruvate (3C)
- Net gain: 2 ATP, 2 NADH
- Does NOT require oxygen

Stage 2 — Pyruvate Oxidation (mitochondrial matrix):
- Each pyruvate → Acetyl-CoA + CO2
- 2 NADH produced

Stage 3 — Krebs Cycle / Citric Acid Cycle (mitochondrial matrix):
- Acetyl-CoA enters cycle
- Produces: 2 ATP, 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, 4 CO2 per glucose

Stage 4 — Electron Transport Chain + Oxidative Phosphorylation (inner mitochondrial membrane):
- NADH and FADH2 donate electrons
- Electrons pass through protein complexes; proton gradient forms
- ATP synthase uses gradient to produce ~34 ATP
- Oxygen is final electron acceptor → forms water

Total ATP yield per glucose molecule: approximately 36–38 ATP
